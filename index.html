A letter to Saints, seekers, and anyone on the shelf

Come and see.

"Can anything good come out of Nazareth?" Philip said to him, "Come and see."

John 1:46

If you've been taught about a Savior, come and see the Jesus of the Bible.

If you're reading this

You're probably either a member of the Church, or you've been meeting with the missionaries.

Maybe you're BIC. Maybe you're a convert. Maybe you served a mission and came home with questions you haven't said out loud. Maybe the shelf is holding, and maybe it isn't. Either way, we're glad you're here. This isn't written in anger, and it isn't written to mock anyone. It's written because something important is at stake. Something worth reading slowly, with an open Bible and a quiet room, without the Gospel Principles manual open beside it.

The question on this page is simple: Is the Savior preached by the Church the same Jesus preached by the apostles in the New Testament? Not "is He similar." Not "does the name match." The same.

We believe, with the gentlest possible conviction, and with receipts drawn from the Standard Works, the correlated manuals, and the words of the Brethren themselves, that the answer is no. And that the difference matters more than almost anything else could.

The apostolic warning

"For if someone comes and proclaims another Jesus than the one we proclaimed, or if you receive a different spirit from the one you received, or a different gospel from the one you accepted, you put up with it readily enough."

2 Corinthians 11:4

Paul wrote these words to a young church being visited by confident, well-spoken traveling preachers who used the same vocabulary he did. His concern was not that they were hostile. His concern was that they were convincing, and that the Jesus they preached was not the Jesus he had preached, even when the name matched.

The receipts

Six places where the restored gospel is not just different from the biblical gospel. It's a different gospel.

01

A Different Savior

The Savior you were taught about was begotten as the firstborn spirit son of Heavenly Father in the pre-existence, the elder brother of every one of us, including Lucifer. The Bible's Jesus is the one and only Son of the one God (John 3:16), appointed as Messiah, with no brotherhood to the adversary.

Read more 02

A Different God

The Lorenzo Snow couplet, "As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may be," is not a forgotten quote. It is the shape of the Plan of Salvation. The Lord declared through Isaiah the exact opposite: "Before me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after me." Either Heavenly Father was once a man on another world, or Isaiah 43 is true. Not both.

Read more 03

A Different Gospel

2 Nephi 25:23, "after all we can do," is the verse every missionary quotes to show the Church believes in grace. But exaltation still requires the ordinances, the garments, the sealing, the temple recommend, tithing settlement, and continuing worthiness. Paul's gospel is that a sinner is justified by grace through faith. Period, not plus.

Read more 04

A Different Scripture

The Standard Works are four books, not one. The Book of Abraham, canonized in the Pearl of Great Price, was translated from papyri the Church now possesses. Egyptologists, LDS and otherwise, agree they are ordinary funerary texts. The Joseph Smith Translation "restored" passages that exist in no manuscript anywhere.

Read more 05

A Different History

The whole structure of the Restoration depends on the Great Apostasy: the claim that after the last apostle died, the true Church was lost to the earth until 1820. But Jesus promised the gates of hell would not prevail against His Church. And the historical record of faithful witness (the martyrs, the councils, the translations, the preserved Scripture) is open for anyone to read.

Read more 06

A Different Prophet

There are nine known accounts of the First Vision. Four are from Joseph Smith himself. They differ on who appeared, on how many appeared, on his age, on what was said. The Kinderhook plates were a hoax he began to translate. Deuteronomy 18 gave Israel a simple test for distinguishing true prophets from false. That test is still in force.

A word before we go further

We know the mission papers. We know the bishop's office. We know what it felt like to bear your testimony, and what it costs to wonder if it was true. We are not asking you to leave. We are asking you to read.

If you are a member

We love you, and we are not your enemy.

We are not asking you to hate the Brethren, tear up your patriarchal blessing, or tell your ward you've apostatized. We are not here to harvest your faith crisis. We know what the shelf feels like, and we know what it costs to look at it straight on.

We are asking one thing: read the Bible without the correlated manual open beside it. Start with the Gospel of John. Ask the Savior who He says He is, in His own words, not summarized through any other scripture. Not this page either. Just Him.

That's all. Not leave. Not decide today. Just read, slowly, and let Him speak for Himself.

If you're meeting with missionaries

Stop. Open the New Testament first.

The elders and sisters sitting in your living room are sincere. They're probably nineteen or twenty, living on a stipend, genuinely convinced that what they're teaching you is the restored gospel. They're telling you that the Church is Christian, that the Book of Mormon is another testament of Jesus Christ, and that you can know it's true by praying about it (Moroni 10:3-5) and feeling a burning in your bosom.

Each of those claims can be tested. The feeling they're asking you to trust is the same feeling sincere people in other faiths have about their own scriptures. The test Jesus actually gave was different: search the Scriptures (John 5:39).

Before you accept a baptismal commitment, spend two weeks alone with the New Testament. Start with John. Ask Jesus who He says He is. Then decide.
