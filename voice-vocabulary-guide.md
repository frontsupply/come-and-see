# Come and See | Voice & Vocabulary Guide

**A writing reference for content that sounds like it knows you.**

---

## Why this matters

The single biggest tell that a piece of LDS-critical writing was written by an outsider is vocabulary. When a born-in-the-covenant member sees "Mormon church," "Mormon god," "the Mormons believe," the piece is filed under _anti-Mormon literature_ within the first paragraph and the reading stops. When they see "the Church," "Heavenly Father," "the Brethren," "we know what the shelf feels like," they keep reading.

This isn't about mimicry or manipulation. It's about naming their lived experience accurately so that when we say _the Jesus of the Bible is not the Savior you've been taught about_, they trust that we actually know who the Savior they've been taught about is.

The goal is a voice that reads like it came from someone raised in the Church who left for Jesus, not from someone who read a Tanner pamphlet.

---

## Core principles

1. **Use their terms, not outsider terms.** "The Church" not "the Mormon church." "Heavenly Father" not just "God." "The Brethren" not "LDS leaders." "Temple" not "their temples."

2. **Capitalize what they capitalize.** The Church. The Gospel. The Restoration. The Atonement. The Plan of Salvation. The Brethren. The Prophet. The First Vision. The Savior.

3. **Name the feelings before you challenge the doctrine.** Every LDS reader has felt the burning in the bosom, has borne testimony, has been in a temple sealing, has held their shelf together. Acknowledge the reality of these experiences before questioning what they mean.

4. **Never mock the temple, the garments, or the sealing.** These are the most sacred things in their life. Critique the doctrine; honor the devotion.

5. **Quote from _their_ sources, not hostile ones.** The Standard Works, _Gospel Principles_, _Preach My Gospel_, _True to the Faith_, General Conference talks, _Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith_. When the receipts come from their own manual, there is no escape hatch.

6. **Don't explain insider terms to insiders.** If we write "the Brethren" and then parenthetically add "(the Quorum of the Twelve and the First Presidency)," we've just announced that we're writing for outsiders. Use the term. Anyone who doesn't know it can context-clue or Google.

7. **For mixed audiences, let the term carry the weight.** Most LDS-insider terms are transparent enough that non-LDS readers can follow. "The Brethren" is obvious from context. "The shelf" might need a sentence to unpack, but that sentence can do double duty. Insider and outsider read it differently.

## What to call things

### The institution and its people

| Outsider word | Insider word | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The Mormon church | The Church | Always capital C, no qualifier needed |
| Mormons | Latter-day Saints, members, Saints | Since 2018, "Mormon" is officially discouraged by the Church itself |
| The LDS | The Church, the Saints | "LDS" is fine but feels bureaucratic |
| Mormon leaders | The Brethren, General Authorities, the Prophet and apostles | "The Brethren" specifically refers to the Q15 |
| Their prophet | The Prophet | Capital P. Current: Russell M. Nelson until further notice |
| Mormon missionaries | The missionaries, the elders, the sisters | "The elders" = missionary males regardless of age |
| Mormon temple | The temple | No qualifier |
| Mormon scriptures | The Standard Works | Bible, Book of Mormon, D&C, Pearl of Great Price |
| Their god | Heavenly Father | In casual speech, rarely just "God" |
| Their jesus | The Savior | Occasionally "Christ," rarely "Jesus" alone |

### Members address each other

"Brother Holm" and "Sister Holm" is the universal form of address at church. "Bishop Smith" or "President Young" for leaders. First names among peers, last names with titles in formal settings.

### Hierarchy from top to bottom

The **First Presidency** is the Prophet plus two counselors. The **Quorum of the Twelve** is the apostles. The **Seventy** is the next tier of general authorities. Below that are **Area Authorities** and **Area Presidency**. The **Stake President** oversees several wards, like a regional director. The **Bishop** runs the local ward, unpaid, serving roughly five years. The **Elders Quorum President**, **Relief Society President**, **Young Men President**, **Young Women President**, and **Primary President** are lay leaders of specific programs within the ward.

A ward has 200 to 500 members. A stake has 5 to 10 wards. A branch is a small ward.

## The lived experience: language that names it

These are the phrases that, more than anything else, mark insider writing. Every LDS reader has experienced these. Naming them correctly is more powerful than any doctrinal argument.

### The shelf

The single most important insider metaphor of the last twenty years. A member's "shelf" is the imagined place where they put things they can't reconcile. The Book of Abraham papyri, the polyandry, the 1978 reversal on the priesthood ban, the racism in early sermons, the changes to the temple ceremony, the Mountain Meadows Massacre. For a while, the shelf holds. Then something, often something small, breaks it, and everything comes down at once.

> "We know what the shelf feels like. We know what it feels like to be afraid of it."

### The burning in the bosom

From D&C 9:8, the spiritual confirmation a member is taught to seek when testing truth. _"You shall feel that it is right."_ Members are told that if they pray about the Book of Mormon (Moroni's promise, Moroni 10:3 through 5), the Holy Ghost will confirm its truth through this inner warmth.

> "We are not dismissing the feeling. We are asking what the feeling actually testifies to."

### Testimony

The public declaration, usually on Fast Sunday, that begins _"I'd like to bear my testimony…"_ and ends with a standardized sequence: _I know the Church is true. I know Joseph Smith was a prophet. I know the Book of Mormon is the word of God. I know that President [name] is a prophet today. I know that we have a Heavenly Father who loves us. In the name of Jesus Christ, amen._

This formula is unmistakable. Echoing it, inverting it, or grieving it are all powerful moves depending on context.

### PIMO: physically in, mentally out

A member who has lost faith but still attends for family or cultural reasons. A huge demographic. Any page that doesn't acknowledge PIMOs is missing perhaps its most important audience, the one already leaning, just waiting for someone to give them permission.

### Faith crisis, faith transition

The internal term for what's happening when the shelf breaks. "I went through a faith crisis at 28." Not "I lost my faith." Not "I deconverted." _Faith crisis_ or _faith transition_.

### CES letter

Jeremy Runnells's 2013 compilation of historical and doctrinal issues, originally written as questions to a CES (Church Educational System) director. Known by name to anyone in or near the exmo or PIMO world. You can reference it once, for signal, without endorsing it as primary source.

### Return with honor

A missionary phrase, the hope that a missionary will complete a mission without moral failure. Broader meaning: that a faithful member will return to the celestial presence of Heavenly Father without disqualifying themselves. A phrase saturated with emotional weight.

### Families can be together forever

From the Primary song. The single emotional anchor of LDS life. Temple sealing is what makes this possible; a member who loses faith is told (or fears) that this is the price. The fear of losing an eternal family is often the largest obstacle to leaving.

> "The promise that families can be together forever is not a promise the Church can keep. But the promise of the gospel, that those who are in Christ are raised with Him, is."

### BIC: born in the covenant

A member born to already-sealed parents, thus already part of the eternal family without needing proxy work. A cultural marker. BICs and converts have somewhat different experiences.

### The veil

Two meanings, both important. First, the forgetting of the pre-mortal life that happens at birth, _"a veil was placed over our memories."_ Second, the physical veil in the temple that the patron passes through. When we say "the veil" in a pastoral context, most members will hear both at once.

## Theological vocabulary

This is where insider voice really sings. The specific doctrinal terms LDS use that don't map cleanly to mainstream Christian vocabulary.

### The Plan of Salvation (or Plan of Happiness)

The full cosmic narrative: pre-mortal existence, birth (veil falls), mortal probation, spirit world, resurrection, judgment, and finally one of three kingdoms of glory (or outer darkness). Every LDS child has drawn this diagram. If we refer to "the Plan" or "the Plan of Salvation," every member knows exactly what we mean.

### Pre-existence, pre-mortal life

Where we all existed as spirit children of Heavenly Father before being born. Every human being (Jesus, Satan, you, me, Hitler) lived there. This is foundational LDS doctrine and has no real parallel in biblical Christianity.

### Exaltation vs. Salvation

Crucial distinction. **Salvation** in LDS teaching is resurrection, given to nearly everyone, independent of works. **Exaltation** is becoming like Heavenly Father, inheriting the celestial kingdom, eternal increase (continuing to produce spirit children). Exaltation requires the full set of ordinances plus continued worthiness.

When the missionaries say "we believe in salvation by grace," they mean something different from what a Baptist means. The word _exaltation_ is the word that does the actual heavy lifting.

### The three degrees of glory

**Celestial** (with Heavenly Father, for the fully exalted), **Terrestrial** (for good non-LDS people and less-valiant members), **Telestial** (for the wicked who eventually repent in the spirit world). Based on D&C 76 and Joseph Smith's reading of 1 Corinthians 15:40-41.

### Outer darkness

Where the **sons of perdition** go. Those who had the truth and denied it. An extremely small category. Not hell in the biblical sense.

### The Godhead

Three separate beings (Heavenly Father, Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost) united in purpose but not in substance. Explicitly not the Trinity. Never use the word "Trinity" as if they held to it.

### Heavenly Parents, Heavenly Mother

Official doctrine. Heavenly Mother is real, she is married to Heavenly Father, she is the mother of our spirits. She is not prayed to. The 2019 Gospel Topics essay _Mother in Heaven_ is the official statement.

### Eternal progression

The doctrine that what Heavenly Father is, we may become. _"As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may be"_ (Lorenzo Snow couplet). Every LDS person recognizes this couplet.

### The Restoration

Capital R. The restoration of the true church through Joseph Smith in 1820 and following. Assumes the Great Apostasy, the total disappearance of the true church after the deaths of the apostles.

### The Great Apostasy

Also capitalized. The claim that the true church, priesthood authority, and true ordinances were lost to the earth from roughly the death of John the Apostle until Joseph Smith's First Vision.

### The First Vision

Joseph Smith's 1820 vision in the Sacred Grove in Palmyra, New York, where (in the canonized 1838 account) Heavenly Father and the Son appeared to him and told him not to join any church, for all were an abomination. Foundational event of Mormonism.

### The priesthood

Always capital-P in context. The authority to act in God's name. LDS distinguishes the **Aaronic** (preparatory, held from age 12 in the old system, now from age 11) and the **Melchizedek** (the higher, held from roughly age 18).

### Ordinances

What other Christians might call sacraments. The required rituals: baptism, confirmation, priesthood ordination, endowment, sealing. Proxy ordinances are performed for the dead by the living.

### Sealing

Eternal binding, performed in the temple. Most commonly marriage sealing, but also child-to-parent sealing. The sealing is what makes a family eternal.

### The endowment

The temple ceremony received first for self, then repeatedly by proxy for the dead. Includes washing and anointing, receiving the garment, receiving the new name, the drama of Adam and Eve, covenants, and passing through the veil. The heart of temple worship.

### The garment

The temple undergarment, worn day and night after receiving the endowment. Marked with specific symbols (compass, square, navel mark, knee mark). _Never_ called "magic underwear" in pastoral writing. That's mocking.

### Temple recommend

The card a member must have, renewed every two years, to enter the temple. Issued after a recommend interview with the bishop and stake president, covering worthiness questions (tithing, Word of Wisdom, chastity, support of the Brethren, and so on).

### Fast Sunday, fast offering, fast and testimony meeting

First Sunday of the month. Members fast two meals and contribute the cost (or more) as a fast offering for the poor. The Sacrament meeting that day is open-mic testimonies instead of prepared talks.

### Sacrament meeting

What other Christians might call Sunday service. Centered on the passing of the Sacrament (bread and water, not wine). Includes talks given by members, not a pastor. The Church has no paid clergy at the local level.

### Worthy, worthiness

An enormous word. Members are "worthy" or "unworthy" of the temple, of the priesthood, of mission service. The system of worthiness interviews runs from early childhood (bishop interviews beginning at age 8, annually) to temple recommend renewal in adulthood.

### Word of Wisdom

D&C 89. The health code. No coffee, no tea (black or green), no alcohol, no tobacco. In practice, also no recreational drugs, no caffeine in some families. Required for a temple recommend.

### Tithing

A full 10% of income. Required for a temple recommend. Tithing settlement happens annually with the bishop, where members declare whether they are "full tithe payers."

## Mission culture vocabulary

Critical to understand. A huge fraction of adult LDS men are returned missionaries (RMs), and the mission is often the most formative period of their life.

**The mish** is the mission. **Companion** or **comp** is the other missionary you live with 24/7. **Senior companion** and **junior companion** is the hierarchical pairing. **Trainer** and **greenie** is the first-transfer missionary under a trainer's wing. **P-day** is preparation day, the one day of the week with reduced rules. **MTC** is the Missionary Training Center (mainly Provo, also Mexico City, São Paulo, and others). **Transfer** is the 6-week cycle; you may be "transferred" to a new area and new companion. **Area, district, zone, mission** is the geographical hierarchy. **Tracting** is door-to-door proselytizing. **Investigator** is someone being taught the discussions. **Golden** or **golden contact** is a highly receptive investigator. **Baptismal commitment** is when someone agrees to be baptized. **Mission president** is the middle-aged couple who oversees all missionaries in the mission; their authority is near-absolute. **Return with honor** means return from your mission without moral disqualification. **Preach My Gospel** is the missionary manual since 2004. **The discussions** are the six prepared lessons taught to investigators. **Splits** means temporarily serving with a different companion for a day. **Zone conference** is the periodic gathering of missionaries in a zone.

## Scripture shorthand

When you cite these, insiders will hear the cadence of verses they've memorized. Use them.

**1 Nephi 3:7** is the "I will go and I will do" verse (the obedience verse). **2 Nephi 25:23** is the grace verse, "We are saved by grace, after all we can do." **Alma 32** is where faith is a seed (the faith chapter). **Moroni 10:3 through 5** is the promise, pray and God will confirm. **3 Nephi 11** is Christ's visit to the Americas. **Moses 1:39** is "This is my work and my glory." **D&C 9:8** is the burning in the bosom. **D&C 76** is the three degrees of glory. **D&C 89** is the Word of Wisdom. **D&C 132** is the revelation on plural marriage (most faithful members have not actually read this). **Joseph Smith-History 1:17 through 19** is the First Vision, the canonized 1838 account.

Every LDS child has sung **"Follow the Prophet,"** **"I Am a Child of God,"** **"Families Can Be Together Forever,"** and **"I Hope They Call Me on a Mission."** You can evoke these song titles in context without explaining them.

## The testimony formula: echoing and inverting

The testimony formula is nearly verbatim across the Church:

> "I'd like to bear my testimony. I know that the Church is true. I know that Joseph Smith was a prophet. I know that the Book of Mormon is the word of God. I know that [current Prophet] is a prophet of God today. I know that we have a Heavenly Father who loves us. In the name of Jesus Christ, amen."

This formula is one of the most recognizable pieces of LDS culture. Pastoral writing can do several things with it:

- **Name it.** "You know the testimony formula. You've said it, probably dozens of times."
- **Grieve it.** "We remember the feeling of saying those words and meaning them."
- **Invert it.** "Here is the testimony we would bear now: We know Jesus is who He says He is. We know the Father is who Jesus said He was. We know the Scriptures are sufficient. In His name, amen."

Handle with care. Never mock.

## Outsider tells to avoid

Phrases that will make a born-in-the-covenant reader bounce within one sentence:

"The Mormon religion" and "the Mormon church" should both be replaced with _the Church_. "Their so-called prophet" should be just _the Prophet_, no scare quotes. "Magic underwear" should be _the garment_, and it is never mocked. "Secret handshakes" should be _the tokens_ or _the grips_. "Kolob" used for cheap jokes should be avoided; reference it only when substantive. "The cult" should be just _the Church_. "Joseph Smith Jr., con man" should be just _Joseph Smith_. Let the record speak. "Sheeple" and "brainwashed" are never acceptable, full stop. Mocking polygamy is off limits; address the history with gravity. Mocking the celestial kingdom or the planets is off limits; the doctrine can be addressed seriously without being mocked. Referring to the Book of Mormon as a novel is off limits; it deserves serious engagement as a claimed scripture, not dismissal.

The rule is this: **Critique the claims; honor the devotion.** Members have given years of their lives, thousands of hours, millions of dollars, and in many cases their entire family identity to this church. Their sincerity deserves respect even when their beliefs don't.

---

## A note on the Restoration framing

One of the most powerful moves available to a pastoral resource like this is reclaiming the word _restoration_. LDS teaching uses it to mean "the restoration of the true church through Joseph Smith." But the biblical witness is that no restoration of a lost church was ever needed. Christ promised the gates of hell would not prevail, and they have not. The apostolic gospel was restored every generation in every place where it was preached, because it never left.

We can gently say: _We believe in a restoration too, but the one the New Testament describes, and the one Jesus promised would never fail._

---

## Final rule

Write like someone who is writing to a sibling they love. Not to a convert you want to rack up. Not to an apostate you want to vindicate. To a sibling. The Church is their home. We are not asking them to burn it down. We are asking them to look, with open Scripture and open heart, at the Jesus of the New Testament, and to come and see.
