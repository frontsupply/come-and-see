# Freemasonry and the Mormon Endowment

**A Research Document for Discernment Ministry**
_Compiled April 2026, TCRM Discernment Research_

---

## Purpose

This document is a ministry-facing research summary of the documented parallels between Masonic Blue Lodge ritual and the Latter-day Saint temple endowment. It is intended for internal reference, pastoral counseling of members coming out of LDS backgrounds, and discernment teaching on syncretism in restorationist movements.

The goal is charity and accuracy. The historical parallels are well-documented by LDS, former-LDS, and neutral academic sources alike, the question is not *whether* there is a relationship, but *what kind* of relationship and what it means theologically.

---

## 1. The Historical Context

### The Nauvoo Timeline

The core timeline is not disputed by any serious historian, LDS or otherwise:

- **March 15, 1842.** Joseph Smith is initiated as an Entered Apprentice at Nauvoo Lodge, Illinois.
- **March 16, 1842.** Joseph Smith is passed and raised to Master Mason the following day. (Most Masons wait months between degrees; Smith received all three degrees in a single day by special dispensation from the Illinois Grand Master.)
- **May 4, 1842.** Seven weeks later, Smith administers the first full endowment ceremony to nine men in the upper room of his Red Brick Store in Nauvoo.

Much of the early leadership was Masonic: Brigham Young, Heber C. Kimball, Hyrum Smith, John Taylor, Willard Richards, Wilford Woodruff, George A. Smith, and others had Masonic backgrounds. James Adams, who was present at the May 4 endowment, was a Deputy Grand Master of Illinois Masonry.

### The Academic Consensus

The strongest single scholarly treatment is Michael W. Homer's 113-page article **"'Similarity of Priesthood in Masonry': The Relationship between Freemasonry and Mormonism"** in *Dialogue: A Journal of Mormon Thought* 27, no. 3 (Fall 1994): 1-113. Homer, a practicing trial lawyer and chair of the Utah Board of State History, is sympathetic to both traditions and concludes that the relationship is real and substantive, not superficial.

Reed C. Durham, Jr., then director of the LDS Institute of Religion adjacent to the University of Utah, delivered his 1974 Mormon History Association presidential address on the topic, concluding that the endowment "had an immediate inspiration from Masonry" and that the similarities were "so apparent and overwhelming that some dependent relationship cannot be denied." He was censured by Church Education System administrators afterward, issued a public apology, and did not participate in the MHA again.

David John Buerger's **_The Mysteries of Godliness: A History of Mormon Temple Worship_** (Smith Research Associates, 1994; reprinted by Signature Books) remains the most thorough historical treatment of the endowment's development. Buerger documents the signs, tokens, keywords, and penalties alongside their Masonic counterparts, while acknowledging there was no wholesale borrowing. Published reviews in *BYU Studies*, even critical ones, concede the factual basis of his reconstruction while disputing his interpretation.

Homer followed up his article with **_Joseph's Temples: The Dynamic Relationship Between Freemasonry and Mormonism_** (University of Utah Press, 2014), which remains the most current full-length academic treatment.

## 2. The Grips / Tokens

The four tokens of the pre-1990 endowment map onto the three Blue Lodge degrees with striking precision. The form of each handgrip is essentially identical; only the covenant names and doctrinal framing differ.

### First Token of the Aaronic Priesthood ↔ Entered Apprentice

**Masonic form:** Right hands joined. The thumb is pressed firmly on the first knuckle of the other person's index finger. In Masonry, the associated word is **"Boaz"** (one of the two pillars of Solomon's temple).

**LDS form:** Identical grip. The associated word is the patron's **New Name**, given during the initiatory. The name varies by day; historically, men received biblical patriarchal names and women received biblical female names.

### Second Token of the Aaronic Priesthood ↔ Fellow Craft

**Masonic form:** Right hands joined. The thumb is pressed into the hollow between the first and second knuckles (i.e., moved one knuckle up from the EA grip). The associated word is **"Shibboleth"** (Judges 12); the grip is also called the grip of "Jachin," the other Solomonic pillar.

**LDS form:** Identical grip. The associated name is the patron's **own given name** (the name under which they were baptized).

### First Token of the Melchizedek Priesthood (Sign of the Nail)

**Masonic form:** There is no exact Blue Lodge parallel for this one at the grip level, some researchers tie it to higher-degree Masonic work. The thumb is placed between the first and second knuckles, applied to the back of the other hand.

**LDS form:** The word is **"Son"** or **"the Son"** (referencing Jesus). This is the first grip of the higher priesthood.

### Second Token of the Melchizedek Priesthood / Patriarchal Grip / Sure Sign of the Nail ↔ Master Mason

This is the tightest parallel and the one that prompted Durham's 1974 address.

**Masonic form (Master Mason):** Known as the Strong Grip of the Lion's Paw or "Mahhah-Bone." Hands are interlocked, fingers curled around the wrist. The two candidates stand on the **Five Points of Fellowship**, foot to foot, knee to knee, breast to breast, hand to back, and mouth to ear. A secret word is communicated mouth-to-ear through this posture. The ritual is framed around the Hiram Abiff legend, the murder of the master architect of Solomon's temple, whose secret name had to be recovered.

**LDS form (pre-1990):** The Patriarchal Grip was given at the veil of the temple. Hands interlocked with little fingers locked and index fingers extended on the wrist. The patron stood opposite a temple worker with a veil between them, in **the same Five Points of Fellowship posture**, foot to foot, knee to knee, breast to breast, hand to back, mouth to ear, and whispered through the veil the exchange: "Health to the navel, marrow to the bones, strength to the loins and sinews, power in the priesthood be upon me and my posterity through all generations of time and throughout all eternity." The whispered name was a secret full name of God.

**This specific posture, the Five Points of Fellowship at the veil, was removed from the endowment in April 1990.** The hand grip itself was retained; the ritual embrace was not. This is the single most decisive piece of evidence that LDS leadership itself recognized the Masonic character of the original form.

## 3. Other Ritual Elements

### The Penalties (removed 1990)

Before 1990, each token in the endowment was paired with a **self-executed penalty**, a gestured pantomime of the punishment for revealing the sign. These mirror the Masonic penalties almost verbatim:

| Degree / Token | Masonic Penalty | LDS Penalty (pre-1990) |
|---|---|---|
| EA / 1st Aaronic | Throat cut across, tongue torn out by the roots | Throat cut from ear to ear, tongue torn out by the roots |
| FC / 2nd Aaronic | Left breast torn open, heart and vitals removed and thrown to beasts of the field | Breast torn open, heart and vitals torn out |
| MM / 1st Melchizedek | Body severed in twain, bowels burned to ashes, ashes scattered to the four winds | Body cut asunder, bowels gushing out |

In each case, a corresponding gesture (a thumb drawn across the throat, across the chest, across the waist) was performed while reciting the covenant. These penalty gestures and their associated oaths were removed in the **April 10, 1990** revision to the endowment, the same revision that removed the Five Points of Fellowship at the veil.

### The Aprons

- **Masonic:** The lambskin apron, worn throughout all ritual work, is the central symbol of the Craft. Each degree has its own ornamentation.
- **LDS:** The fig-leaf apron is placed on each patron during the endowment drama (when Adam and Eve are expelled from Eden) and worn throughout. In pre-1990 ceremonies it was green silk with a nine-leaf pattern.

### The Compass and Square Marks

The temple garment has historically been marked with:
- A **compass** symbol over the right breast
- A **square** symbol over the left breast
- A horizontal mark over the navel (the "mark of the navel")
- A vertical mark over the right knee (the "mark of the knee")

The compass and square are the core Masonic symbols. LDS sources describe them as reminders of covenants. Masonic sources describe them as representing the virtues of conduct and the bounds of morality, respectively.

### The Veil

In both rituals, progression through the ceremony culminates at a **veil**, which the candidate must pass through by correctly giving the signs, tokens, and words. In Masonry this is sometimes represented by the Royal Arch ritual (beyond Blue Lodge); in the LDS endowment it is the veil of the temple separating the instruction rooms from the celestial room.

### Oaths of Secrecy

Both traditions require the candidate to swear on severe self-executing penalty never to reveal the signs and tokens. The LDS covenants of secrecy remain (reworded) post-1990; only the physical penalty gestures and verbal penalty language were removed.

## 4. What the 1990 Revision Tells Us

On April 10, 1990, the LDS Church implemented the most substantial revision to the endowment since Brigham Young's time. The changes included:

- Removal of all three penalty gestures and their associated oaths
- Removal of the Five Points of Fellowship at the veil
- Removal of the "Oath of Vengeance" (this had been removed earlier, in 1927, following congressional scrutiny during the Reed Smoot hearings)
- Significant softening of the portrayal of the Protestant minister character ("the sectarian minister hired by Lucifer")
- Reframing of women's covenants (previously women covenanted obedience to their husbands "as he obeys the Lord"; revised to covenant directly with the Lord)

These are precisely the elements most recognizably Masonic or culturally embarrassing. The fact that the Church revised them indicates institutional awareness that these elements presented a problem. The official explanation has emphasized continuing revelation and adaptation, not acknowledgment of Masonic derivation.

## 5. Two Interpretive Frameworks

### The LDS Apologetic Position

The standard faithful LDS response is the **"corrupted restoration"** argument: Masonry preserves fragments of an original Solomonic, or even Adamic, temple ritual that has been handed down in corrupted form through the centuries. Joseph Smith, receiving revelation, was able to restore the original pure form. Apparent borrowing is therefore actually restoration of a shared ancient source.

The faithful version is developed in works like Matthew B. Brown's *Exploring the Connection Between Mormons and Masons* and various articles in *BYU Studies*.

**Weaknesses of this position:**
- Modern speculative Freemasonry is well-documented as originating in 17th-18th century Britain (David Stevenson, *The Origins of Freemasonry*, Cambridge University Press, 1988). There is no historical continuity back to Solomon's temple; the Hiram Abiff legend is a post-Reformation invention.
- The specific grips, words, and penalties of Blue Lodge Masonry are known innovations with traceable development in English and Scottish lodges.
- The argument requires simultaneously that Masonry preserves ancient truth *and* that it is so corrupted as to need restoration, a circular move that cannot be falsified.

### The Historical-Critical Position

The simpler explanation: Joseph Smith, newly initiated into Masonry, adapted its ritual forms for the endowment he introduced seven weeks later. He believed (as did many nineteenth-century Americans) that Masonry preserved ancient mysteries; he saw himself as authorized to restore and perfect them. The borrowing was conscious but theologically reframed.

This is essentially Homer's position, Buerger's position, and the position of most non-LDS historians.

## 6. Theological Assessment

From a biblical unitarian and restorationist perspective, several concerns emerge regardless of which historical framework one accepts:

**1. Oath-bound secrecy is foreign to New Testament Christianity.** Jesus explicitly teaches in Matthew 5:33-37 not to swear oaths at all, "let your yes be yes and your no be no; anything beyond this comes from the evil one." Paul's ministry is consistently described as open and public (Acts 26:26, "this thing was not done in a corner"). The entire pattern of a private ritual with self-executed penalty oaths is structurally incompatible with Christ's teaching.

**2. Self-executing penalty oaths invoke curses on the covenanter.** Whether Masonic or LDS, the structure asks the candidate to call down physical destruction on themselves if they reveal the secret. This is closer to ancient Near Eastern suzerainty curses than to biblical covenant.

**3. Temple ritual after the cross is questionable on biblical grounds.** Hebrews 9-10 argues sustainedly that the Levitical temple ritual was a shadow fulfilled in Christ, who entered "once for all" into the true sanctuary. The restoration of an elaborate priestly ritual with handclasp passwords and veil exchanges represents a conceptual reversal of the book of Hebrews' central argument.

**4. The endowment's "true name of God" secret is theologically striking.** In the pre-1990 veil ceremony, the patron receives a secret name of God, whispered through the veil, that is said to be necessary for admission into the celestial kingdom. This is in tension with Romans 10:9-13, where calling openly on the name of the Lord Jesus is sufficient for salvation and is available to all.

**5. The structural resemblance to mystery religion initiation is notable.** The pattern of progressive revelation, secret passwords, guarded handgrips, and graded advancement through ritual gates is the pattern of the ancient mystery cults (Eleusis, Mithras, Isis), not the pattern of the New Covenant, which is characterized by open proclamation of a gospel to the whole world.

## 8. Pastoral Notes

When working with members coming out of LDS backgrounds, several practical points deserve emphasis:

- The temple experience is often deeply emotionally formative, and dismissing it abruptly can cause harm. The covenants are felt as sacred even when the content is recognized as syncretistic.
- Former LDS members are often bound by residual anxiety about the penalty oaths, even post-1990. Pastoral assurance from Matthew 5:33-37 (Christ's explicit release from oath-taking) and from the finished work of the cross is more helpful than polemic.
- The Patriarchal Grip through the veil is, for many LDS, the emotional high point of their temple experience. Handling this with gravity and care, not mockery, is essential.
- The parallel to the mystery religions should be drawn gently; the goal is helping someone see the pattern, not shaming them for having been inside it.

## Summary

The historical and ritual relationship between Masonic Blue Lodge degrees and the pre-1990 LDS endowment is documented, substantial, and acknowledged by responsible scholars in both traditions. The four tokens of the endowment map onto the three Blue Lodge degrees at the level of handgrip form, covenant-oath structure, penalty gestures, and progression through a veil. The 1990 revision removed the most recognizably Masonic elements.

The LDS apologetic framing reads this as restoration of a shared ancient source. The historical-critical framing reads it as nineteenth-century adaptation. In either case, the resulting ritual pattern, oath-bound secrecy, graded handgrip advancement, self-executing penalty covenants, and whispered divine names as admission passwords, is difficult to reconcile with New Testament ecclesiology and with the completed priestly work of Christ as argued in Hebrews.

For the discerning Christian, the question is not primarily *where did this come from historically* but *does this pattern conform to the gospel of Jesus Christ as openly proclaimed in the apostolic writings*. The answer, on careful examination, is that it does not.
