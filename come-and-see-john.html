Start with Jesus

Seven days in the Gospel of John.

Not a study manual. Not a commentary. Not a checklist. Just the Savior, in His own words, with one quiet question each day to carry with you.

This is the oldest prayer in the book. Whoever you are, whatever you have been taught, whatever you still believe or don't, the Savior is not afraid of your questions, and He is not in a hurry. Seven days is long enough to read the Gospel of John carefully, and short enough that you can begin tonight.

The instructions are simple. You do not need this site open while you read. You do not need a Mormon, evangelical, or Orthodox commentary beside you. You need a Bible, a quiet room, and a willingness to let Jesus speak for Himself.

Seven small rules

  1. Use the Bible you have. The King James Version you likely own is fine. Any standard English translation will get you through the Gospel of John safely. If you want a translation that is particularly careful about the points this site is making, our Resources page mentions one. For this week, do not worry about it. Read what you own.
  2. Read the whole section in one sitting. Don't skip around. Let the Gospel do its work in order, the way John wrote it.
  3. Read slowly. Out loud, if you can. The Gospel of John was written to be heard.
  4. Close other books. No Institute manual. No Come Follow Me guide. No correlated material. No evangelical commentary. Just the text.
  5. Pray before you read. Heavenly Father knows the one you are looking for. Ask Him, in the name of His Son, to speak to you through the Word. Then open the book.
  6. Underline, circle, write in the margins. Your Bible is not holy because it is unmarked. It is holy because it is God's Word to you.
  7. Take the quiet question into your day. Each day below ends with one. Carry it into your next meeting, your drive, your dishes, your sleep. Let it breathe.
Day One

The Word became flesh.

Read: John 1 (approximately 6 minutes)

John opens his Gospel with language that reaches back to the very first words of Genesis. In the beginning. God speaks, and worlds come into being. God speaks, and life is called forth. The Word is God's own self-expression, His creative speech, His purpose going out into the world. And then, John tells us something astonishing: that Word became flesh, and walked among us, full of grace and truth. The Savior is the living expression of the Father's heart.

Watch how the chapter moves. John the Baptist points to Him and calls Him the Lamb of God. The first disciples start following Him. Andrew brings his brother. And then Philip finds Nathanael under a fig tree, and invites him with the three words that gave this whole site its name.

Come and see. That's the whole chapter. That's the whole book.

Who does the Savior say He is in this chapter? Listen for the names others call Him, and for what He answers.

Day Two

Signs and conversations.

Read: John 2 to 4 (approximately 15 minutes)

Three chapters, three unforgettable scenes. Water turned to wine at a wedding that was running dry. A whip of cords in the temple, overturning the tables of people who had made a profit business out of the things of God. A midnight conversation with a religious leader named Nicodemus, who came quietly because he could not ask his questions in public. A noon conversation with a Samaritan woman, who had asked her questions loudly for years and never found answers.

Watch Jesus with Nicodemus. He speaks plainly. You must be born again. Watch Him with the woman at the well. He speaks plainly there too. I who speak to you am He. He is not evasive. He is not partial. He tells both of them who He is.

Where in your life are you Nicodemus? Where are you the woman at the well? Let Jesus speak to both.

Day Three

The bread of life.

Read: John 5 and 6 (approximately 14 minutes)

A man lies paralyzed at the pool of Bethesda for thirty-eight years. Jesus sees him. Jesus heals him. The religious authorities are furious, because it is the Sabbath. In the conversation that follows, Jesus says something that should stop you in your tracks. You search the Scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that bear witness about me.

Then, in chapter six, He feeds five thousand people from a boy's lunch. The crowd follows Him across the lake, looking for more bread. He tells them He is the bread. That if they do not eat of this bread, they have no life in them. It is a hard saying. Many of His disciples walk away. Peter, when asked if he wants to leave too, says the only answer that matters: Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life.

What would it mean to say what Peter said? Let that one settle in.

Day Four

I am the light.

Read: John 7 to 9 (approximately 17 minutes)

Jesus teaches in the temple at the Feast of Tabernacles. He forgives a woman caught in adultery. He declares Himself the light of the world. He debates with the religious authorities, who keep insisting they know who He is and who He isn't. And then He does something specific in chapter eight. He says, Before Abraham was, I am.

That is not casual language. Jesus is claiming something far larger than the LDS frame of Him as the firstborn spirit brother among billions. He is saying that in the plan and purposes of God, He is older than Abraham, anchored in the very beginning of God's saving work. The crowd hears the weight of the claim. They pick up stones.

And then, in chapter nine, He heals a man born blind. When the man is questioned by the religious authorities, he cannot answer their hard theological questions. But he can answer this one. One thing I know, that though I was blind, now I see.

What is the one thing you know? Not the testimony you bore. The one thing you know.

Day Five

The Good Shepherd.

Read: John 10 to 12 (approximately 18 minutes)

Jesus speaks of Himself as the Good Shepherd, the one who lays down His life for the sheep. He says His sheep hear His voice, and He knows them, and they follow Him. He says no one can snatch them out of His hand, and no one can snatch them out of His Father's hand.

Then His friend Lazarus dies. Jesus comes to Bethany, weeps with Martha and Mary, and calls the man out of the tomb. I am the resurrection and the life. Six days before the Passover, Mary pours perfume on His feet and dries them with her hair. The crowd's hosannas fill Jerusalem. Jesus knows the hour has come. He tells His disciples as plainly as He can. He is about to die.

Listen in chapter twelve for the voice from heaven. It speaks, and most of the crowd hears only thunder. A few hear the actual voice. Which one were you the last time you prayed?

Do you know the voice of the Good Shepherd? What does it sound like in your life?

Day Six

The upper room.

Read: John 13 to 17 (approximately 22 minutes)

This is the heart of the Gospel of John. Five chapters in an upper room, on the last night before the cross. Jesus washes the disciples' feet. Judas goes out into the night. Jesus tells them He is leaving, and they do not understand. He tells them He is the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through Him.

Pay attention to what He does not say. He does not say the way includes ordinances yet to be revealed. He does not say it includes temples yet to be built. He does not say it includes a prophet yet to be born. He says I am the way. Period.

In chapter seventeen He prays to the Father in a way that lets you listen in on how the Son talks to the Father when no one is watching. Read it carefully. It is the most intimate passage in the Bible. And remember while you read it that He is praying for you. Not for these only do I ask, but also for those who will believe in me through their word. That is you.

Jesus prayed for you before you were born. What did He ask the Father on your behalf?

Day Seven

It is finished.

Read: John 18 to 21 (approximately 22 minutes)

The garden. The arrest. Peter's denial in the courtyard, three times, before the rooster crowed. Pilate, with the wearied question he is still known for. What is truth? The cross. The three words that change everything. It is finished.

Hold onto those three words. In the Greek the word is tetelestai. It is a word that was used for debts. Paid in full. The Savior did not say, it is begun, now the ordinances must be added. He did not say, the work continues in those who are worthy. He said the debt was paid. Fully. Finally.

And then, in the morning, the tomb is empty. Mary sees Him first. The disciples see Him in the upper room. Thomas touches the wounds. And then, by the lake at sunrise, Jesus cooks breakfast for the men who abandoned Him and asks Peter, three times, do you love me. He does not ask about worthiness. He asks about love. And then He says, feed my sheep.

That's the end of the Gospel. That's the last word. The risen Savior at breakfast, asking you to love Him.

What would you say if He asked you that question this morning? Not what you should say. What you would actually say. Answer Him.

After the seven days

Now what?

There is no required next step. The Savior did not give Peter a set of ordinances to complete by Tuesday. He asked him to love Him, and He asked him to feed the sheep. Those two things are the rest of the Christian life, and they will unfold for you in a way that no website can predict or prescribe.

If you want to read another Gospel, try Luke next. If you want to read Paul, start with Ephesians. If you want to understand what the apostles preached after Pentecost, read the book of Acts straight through. The Bible you already own is sufficient. You do not need another book.

If you are looking for people to walk with, we want to be honest: not every church that calls itself Christian holds to the same Savior you met in the Gospel of John. Some have added layers of tradition, creed, or philosophy that would have been as strange to the apostles as they are to you. Others have kept to the Bible alone, hold to the one God (the Father), and see Jesus as His appointed Messiah and risen Son. Those are the fellowships we would point you toward. Our Resources page lists a few ministries that can help you find them. Email us and we can help you look.

If you are still in the Church, we are not asking you to announce anything. We are not asking you to leave on any particular timeline. We are asking you to keep reading. The Savior who met you in the Gospel of John will not leave you now. I will never leave you or forsake you, He said. He meant it. He still does.

If you want to talk to someone, the email at the bottom of this page is real. A person reads what comes in. There is no system, no pipeline, no conversion funnel on the other end of it. Just a couple in East Tennessee who wrote this site because they love the Saints and love the Savior, and who would be honored to hear from you.
