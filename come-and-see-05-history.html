05 / Six Differences

A Different History.

The whole structure of the Restoration depends on an earlier collapse. If the gates of hell did prevail, and the true Church disappeared entirely after the death of the last apostle, then Joseph Smith's mission makes sense. If they did not, and it did not, then what was he restoring?

What the Church teaches

The Great Apostasy is not a minor doctrine.

It is the foundation under the foundation. Every claim the Church makes about its own necessity begins here. The original apostles were killed. The authority they carried, the Melchizedek priesthood, died with them. The ordinances were corrupted. The doctrine was muddled. The church councils of the fourth century, producing the Nicene Creed and the later formulations, enshrined philosophical error where revealed truth had been. The result, according to LDS teaching, was a total apostasy that lasted roughly eighteen hundred years.

In that long darkness, there was no true church on the earth. There were sincere believers, yes. There were faithful hearts. But there was no priesthood authority, no properly administered ordinances, no prophet, no living revelation. The best of the historic Christian traditions were holding onto fragments of the truth, but the whole was gone. God was waiting for the fullness of times, when He would restore what had been lost.

That restoration began in 1820 in the Sacred Grove in Palmyra, when fourteen-year-old Joseph Smith prayed to ask which of the churches he should join. He was told, in the canonized account, that he should join none of them.

The receipts

From the Prophet's own canonized account.

Joseph Smith-History 1:19

"I was answered that I must join none of them, for they were all wrong; and the Personage who addressed me said that all their creeds were an abomination in his sight; that those professors were all corrupt; that: 'they draw near to me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me, they teach for doctrines the commandments of men, having a form of godliness, but they deny the power thereof.'"
Joseph Smith-History 1:19, Pearl of Great Price, canonized 1880.

Notice what is being claimed here. Not that the historic Christian churches had drifted on minor points. Not that they needed reform on some doctrines. The personage identified as the Lord tells Joseph that all their creeds are an abomination. Every one. Protestant, Catholic, Orthodox. The people who professed Christ in those traditions are all corrupt.

This is what the Church means by the Great Apostasy. It is total. It is the condition that makes restoration necessary. Without it, there is nothing to restore.

The Bible

The Savior made a promise that speaks directly to this claim.

When Peter confessed that Jesus was the Christ, the Son of the living God, the Savior's response was a promise about the Church He was going to build. Not a hope. Not a plan. A promise.

"And I tell you, you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it."

Matthew 16:18

The structure of the sentence matters. Jesus does not say the gates of hell will probably not prevail, or that they will not prevail for a season. He says they will not prevail. Period. The Greek is a strong negative. The promise is absolute.

If the gates of hell prevailed for eighteen centuries, then this verse is not true. If this verse is true, then the gates of hell did not prevail, which means the true Church was not entirely lost. Both cannot hold at once.

The promise is reinforced a few chapters later, at the end of Matthew, when the Savior sends the apostles to make disciples of all nations.

"And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."

Matthew 28:20

Always. To the end of the age. Not for four centuries and then eighteen more centuries of absence. Always.

Paul tells the Ephesians that glory is given to God in the church and in Christ Jesus throughout all generations, forever and ever (Ephesians 3:21). Again the structure. All generations. Not some generations, with a long gap in the middle. All.

"I will ask the Father, and he will give you another Helper, to be with you forever, even the Spirit of truth."

John 14:16-17

The Helper is not said to depart for eighteen centuries. The Helper remains. The Spirit is given, and by the Spirit the faithful have been preserved, generation after generation, without interruption.

The historical record

The witnesses to an unbroken gospel are not hidden.

You do not have to take this on faith. The record is open. It is messy, it includes real failure and real corruption, but the record of Christian witness is continuous from the first century to the present, and a great deal of it is not what the Great Apostasy narrative leads you to expect.

Ignatius of Antioch, writing around 110 AD on his way to be martyred, clings to the Savior and the unity of the Church. Justin Martyr in the second century defends the faith to the Roman emperor, articulating the resurrection, baptism, and the hope of the kingdom in terms that a careful Bible reader today can still recognize. Irenaeus in the late second century writes Against Heresies, defending the apostolic tradition against early Gnostic distortions, citing the four Gospels by name, calling the Church back to Scripture. These men were not perfect teachers, but they were not corrupt. They were bleeding for Jesus.

The fourth century brought changes that biblical Christians in every generation since have had to sort through carefully. When Constantine took Christianity under the wing of the Roman state in 313, and when the great councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381) wrote creeds that went considerably beyond what the New Testament itself had said about the nature of the Father and the Son, something real did happen to the shape of church teaching. We do not accept the LDS claim that all true witness disappeared in that century. We do believe the institutional church, backed by imperial power, over time added doctrinal formulations the apostles themselves would not have recognized, and punished those who kept asking to be shown these doctrines from Scripture alone.

The evidence of that faithful remnant is scattered but visible. Through the long medieval period, believers who kept reading the Scriptures in their own languages (the Waldensians, the Hussites, the Lollards) preached a gospel of grace before the Reformation ever began. Wycliffe translated the Bible into English and was persecuted for it. Tyndale did the same and was strangled and burned for it.

The Protestant Reformation recovered much, especially on grace and the sufficiency of Scripture. But the Reformers kept the fourth-century creedal formulations largely intact. It fell to later movements, the Polish Brethren in the sixteenth century, the early English Unitarians and General Baptists in the seventeenth, the American Restoration Movement in the nineteenth (including what became the Church of God General Conference, the Churches of Christ, and related fellowships), to ask the harder question: what does the Bible itself, without the creeds, actually teach about the Father, about Jesus, about the gospel?

These were not fringe movements. They were believers, often at cost to themselves, insisting on the full force of the Shema (the LORD our God, the LORD is one) and on Jesus as the appointed Son, the Messiah of Israel, the risen Lord, without the later philosophical overlay. Their witness is part of the continuous line the gates of hell did not prevail against.

And in every century, in every region, faithful men and women died rather than deny the Savior. They went to the lions, to the stakes, to the gallows. They were not waiting for a nineteenth-century restoration. They were already with Jesus.

The difference

If the promise of Jesus is true, no new restoration was needed.

This is the heart of it. If the gates of hell did not prevail, then there has always been a remnant holding to the gospel, hidden in some eras, visible in others, always imperfect, always composed of sinners, always built on Christ, never lost. And the Great Apostasy as the LDS Church describes it, a total disappearance of true Christianity requiring a nineteenth-century prophet to restore it, is not a historical event. It is a theological claim invented to justify a newer one.

We can believe, gently and with charity, that Joseph Smith was sincere. We can grant that the nineteenth-century American religious landscape was a mess of revivalism, denominational competition, and genuine spiritual hunger. We can understand why a young man in that context would wonder which church to join, and why an answer that said none of them would feel simpler and more satisfying than the patient historical work of discerning faithful witness inside and outside the major traditions. But we cannot, without rejecting the plain words of the Savior, believe that the Church He promised to build was entirely absent for eighteen centuries.

A word to the one reading

You have brothers and sisters in the faith that you did not know you had.

One of the quietest losses in LDS life is the sense that the Christian world outside the Church was just noise. Protestant neighbors, Catholic grandparents, the evangelical radio preacher. All of them, according to the First Vision account, were all corrupt. Their creeds, all an abomination. Their worship, a form of godliness denying the power thereof. It is hard to love someone across a chasm like that, and many Saints have tried anyway, out of sheer good faith.

The chasm is not what you were told it was. Across the centuries, and still today, there are believers who have held to the one God (the Father), to Jesus as His appointed Son and Messiah, to the Scriptures as sufficient, to the coming kingdom as the real hope. Not all of them are easy to find. Some are small fellowships. Some are individual families reading the Bible together at the kitchen table. Some are whole movements you were never told about in seminary or in Sunday School.

You are not alone. You are coming home to something older than the Restoration and older than the Reformation and older than the creeds. You are coming home to the Scriptures themselves, and to the Savior they testify about.

Come and see.
